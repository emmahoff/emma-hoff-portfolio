01 / Why

“Why?” was my favorite question as a child, and it never really stopped being one. I owe that curiosity in large part to my grandfather, a diesel mechanic who let me spend countless hours with him in his workshop, hands covered in grease, taking things apart just to understand how they worked and putting them back together to make sure I actually did. That workshop is where I first fell in love with engines, and where I realized that engineering wasn’t just a subject to study — it was a way of thinking I already had.

Since then, my path has been clear: I am currently a mechanical engineering student, with my sights set on a career in Formula 1 and high-performance automotive engineering, building toward internship opportunities across the world. But my curiosity about how things work has always extended beyond machines. As a child I was equally captivated by nature and animals, and that early love has grown into a genuine drive to work in environments where engineering can be part of the solution to protecting our planet. I spend much of my free time watching documentaries and reading research on innovations tackling global warming, because I believe the same problem-solving mindset that takes an engine apart can also be turned toward building a more sustainable future.

That combination is what draws me to the opportunities I pursue. I'm drawn to engineering challenges that are complex and high-performance by nature — systems built to run reliably under real demands — while also caring about the impact they have on the world they operate in. Whether that system is a suspension component, an automated production line, or something far larger in scale, the instinct is the same one that first pulled me toward my grandfather's engines: take it apart, understand it properly, and make it better.
